## Meta Requirements

### Staff Engineer Alignment Checklist

Staff engineering writing demonstrates cross-team technical leadership. Your writing should:

- [ ] **Systems thinking** — Consider second and third-order effects
- [ ] **Organizational awareness** — How technical decisions affect teams, not just code
- [ ] **Trade-off articulation** — Clear about what you're optimizing for and what you're sacrificing
- [ ] **Pattern recognition** — Connect specific instances to broader principles
- [ ] **Scope calibration** — Know when to solve locally vs. globally
- [ ] **Enablement focus** — How your work enables others, not just your own output

**Staff Engineer Voice Examples:**
- ✅ "This pattern keeps recurring across teams. Here's how we approached it..."
- ✅ "We optimized for X, which meant accepting Y. Here's why that tradeoff made sense..."
- ✅ "The technical decision was straightforward; the organizational challenge was..."
- ❌ "I built this cool thing" (without broader context)
- ❌ "The right answer is obviously..." (without acknowledging alternatives)
- ❌ "My team did X" (without organizational awareness)

---

### Staff+ Signals to Demonstrate

- [ ] Cross-team impact and coordination
- [ ] Technical strategy, not just tactics
- [ ] Mentorship and enablement of others
- [ ] Long-term thinking and sustainability
- [ ] Navigating organizational complexity
- [ ] Knowing when NOT to build something

## Post Structure

Follow the **Context → Problem Framing → Approach → Execution → Impact → Learnings** flow.

---

### Title

Use titles that signal strategic thinking:
- "Scaling [X]: Lessons from [Context]"
- "[Pattern] at [Scale]: A Staff Engineer's Perspective"
- "Why We [Decision]: The Technical Strategy Behind [Outcome]"
- "Building for [N] Teams: [Topic]"

---

### Hook (100-150 words)

- What's the strategic challenge?
- Why does it matter beyond your immediate team?
- What will readers learn?

---

### 1. Context (300-400 words)

#### 1.1 The Landscape
- Organizational context (scale, teams, constraints)
- Where this problem sits in the broader technical ecosystem
- Why this became a priority

#### 1.2 Stakeholders
- Who is affected by this problem?
- Competing interests and priorities
- Political/organizational dynamics (tastefully)

#### 1.3 Constraints
- Technical constraints
- Organizational constraints
- Resource/time constraints
- What made this hard?

---

### 2. Problem Framing (300-400 words)

#### 2.1 The Surface Problem
- What people initially said the problem was
- Symptoms vs. root causes

#### 2.2 The Actual Problem
- What investigation revealed
- Why the obvious solution wouldn't work
- The deeper challenge

#### 2.3 Success Criteria
- How you defined success
- Metrics and qualitative goals
- What "good enough" looked like

**[FRAMING NOTE]**
> The hardest part of staff engineering is often problem framing, not problem solving. Solving the wrong problem well is still failure.

---

### 3. Approach (400-500 words)

#### 3.1 Options Considered
- Option A: [Description, pros, cons]
- Option B: [Description, pros, cons]
- Option C: [Description, pros, cons]

#### 3.2 Decision Framework
- How you evaluated options
- What you optimized for
- What you explicitly deprioritized

#### 3.3 The Chosen Path
- What you decided and why
- Key tradeoffs accepted
- How you built consensus

#### 3.4 What You Chose NOT to Do
- Scope you cut and why
- Problems you explicitly deferred
- "Not now" decisions

---

### 4. Execution (400-500 words)

#### 4.1 Technical Implementation
- High-level architecture
- Key technical decisions
- Code/system examples where helpful

#### 4.2 Organizational Execution
- How you coordinated across teams
- Communication strategy
- Handling disagreements

#### 4.3 Iteration and Adaptation
- What changed as you learned more
- Pivot points
- How you managed scope

#### 4.4 Enabling Others
- How you brought others along
- Documentation/training created
- Knowledge transfer approach

---

### 5. Impact (300-400 words)

#### 5.1 Direct Outcomes
- Metrics improvements
- Problems solved
- Teams unblocked

#### 5.2 Indirect Outcomes
- Capabilities enabled
- Patterns established
- Precedents set

#### 5.3 Organizational Impact
- Cross-team effects
- Cultural shifts
- Process improvements

#### 5.4 What Didn't Work
- Outcomes that fell short
- Unexpected consequences
- Course corrections needed

---

### 6. Learnings (300-400 words)

#### 6.1 Technical Learnings
- What you'd do differently technically
- Patterns that emerged
- Principles validated or invalidated

#### 6.2 Organizational Learnings
- What you learned about coordination
- Communication that worked/didn't
- Stakeholder management insights

#### 6.3 Personal Growth
- How this changed your thinking
- Skills developed
- Perspective shifts

#### 6.4 Advice for Others
- "If you're facing a similar situation..."
- Common pitfalls
- Key questions to ask

---

### 7. Open Questions (150-200 words)

- What remains unsolved?
- Where does this approach break down?
- What would you investigate with more time?

---

### Conclusion (100-150 words)

- Summary of key insight
- Broader applicability
- Call to action or reflection prompt

## Writing Notes

### Staff Engineer Guidelines

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Show systems thinking | Focus only on your code |
| Acknowledge tradeoffs | Present as obvious decisions |
| Credit collaborators | Take sole credit |
| Discuss failures | Only show successes |
| Consider org dynamics | Ignore political reality |
| Enable others | Hoard knowledge |

### Scope Indicators

Your writing should demonstrate awareness of:
- Quarters/years, not just sprints
- Multiple teams, not just yours
- Organization strategy, not just project goals
- Career enablement, not just task completion

### Humility Balance

- Confident about your reasoning
- Humble about your conclusions
- Clear about what you don't know
- Open to alternative approaches
